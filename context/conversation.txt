How do you recreate the feeling of one puzzle in your hands, one piece of paper, and a master guide watching — on a phone screen, without a master guide physically present?
That is THE question. Everything else is downstream.
My recommendation: The "One Puzzle, One Screen" Principle
Stop trying to show the chatbot, puzzle, and understanding document at the same time. That's the paper-vs-screen mistake. On paper you don't have three boxes competing — you have one page where thinking flows freely. The app should work the same way.
Here's the model: the puzzle is the whole screen, always. Everything else is ambient or on-demand.

The puzzle (with its generated image) IS the background/canvas
The user's thoughts stream inline, like notes on the page
The elements aren't a sidebar — they're summonable (swipe, tap, or pull up from bottom)
The nudge isn't a chatbot — it's a whisper that appears in the margin when requested
The understanding document isn't a panel — it's a separate view you swipe to when you want to see what you've built
The whole screen subtly shifts color/atmosphere based on which element is active (Fire tints the background slightly warmer-red, Water cooler-blue, etc.) — but always within your palette

This is not a dashboard. This is a puzzle in your hand.
The magic moment — the thing that makes this unforgettable — is when the puzzle image itself begins to shift as you think. The vision, as I heard it:
User clicks a puzzle card. The screen takes over — a 5-to-10-second cinematic CSS animation plays, like the intro cutscene of Uncharted 4. Story-driven. The puzzle visually assembles itself. All UI is disabled during this moment — the user is inside the puzzle, not looking at a dashboard. Then the puzzle text types itself onto the screen. The animation settles into an ambient state.
Now the user is alone with the puzzle. A frosted glass panel at the bottom — not a chatbot, just a place to think out loud. Write or speak (voice-to-text). A subtle button on the left opens the Understanding Document as an overlay. A subtle button on the bottom-right requests a nudge. No sidebar. No three-column dashboard. One puzzle, one screen, one mind.
That's the product. Let me stress-test it with you before writing any spec.
What's right about this direction:
The cinematic intro solves the "this feels like a dashboard" problem instantly. It establishes: you are entering an experience, not opening an app. The frosted glass thinking panel solves the "chatbot feels clinical" problem. The button-triggered overlays solve the "too much on screen" problem. Voice-to-text solves the "typing kills flow" problem you raised. And CSS animations keep you out of heavy image-gen territory for MVP — fast, cheap, no API costs per puzzle.
What I want to pressure-test:

The animation is per-puzzle, not per-session. If the animation takes 10 seconds and the user comes back to the same puzzle 5 times, that 10-second intro becomes a wall they hit every time. Options: play it only on first open, add a "skip" after 2 seconds, or make it faster on re-entry. Which?
"The user doesn't know this is a chatbot." This is the most interesting line you wrote. Lean into it. The frosted glass panel should feel like a thinking space, not a messaging app. No message bubbles. No "send" arrow. Maybe just a cursor blinking on the glass with a subtle prompt like "What do you see?" or "Think out loud." When they write, their words stay on the glass — they're not sent off to a bot, they're kept there like notes. The nudge, when requested, appears as a whisper in a different typographic style — maybe italicized, in a different corner of the screen. Not a reply. A whisper.
The Understanding Document needs to be "very very very very accurate to what the user said." You emphasized that four times. Important. The current extraction prompt pulls insights in Claude's voice. You want it in the user's voice — their actual language, their phrasing, just organized. The extraction prompt needs to become: "Pull the key realization from what the user wrote. Use their own words where possible. Do not rephrase — organize." That's a different prompt than what's in the current spec.
The CSS animation storyline — who writes it? For MVP, you probably want ~6 Edward Burger puzzles, each with a custom cinematic intro. That's 6 animations to design. Are you going to direct each one (describe the scene, Claude writes the CSS), or do you want a generic "assembly" animation that works for any puzzle with the puzzle text composed last?

My recommendation: one master animation pattern that works for all puzzles, with the puzzle-specific element being the final composition. Example: geometric red and blue shapes fly in from offscreen, collide and arrange themselves into an abstract representation of the puzzle, then the puzzle text types itself in the center. Every puzzle uses the same animation vocabulary but the final shape composition is different. This way you ship 6 puzzles without designing 6 separate cutscenes. Later, when the product works, you invest in per-puzzle custom animations.

The nudge — margin, not reply. You said "click on the bottom right, the LLM prompts them depending on the route of understanding they've built." Perfect. But the nudge should NOT appear in the frosted glass panel alongside the user's thoughts. That would pollute their thinking space. The nudge should appear elsewhere — maybe fading in at the top-right as a handwritten-style whisper, maybe as a small card that slides in from the side. The user's thinking and the nudge need to feel like different voices in different places, not a conversation thread.
