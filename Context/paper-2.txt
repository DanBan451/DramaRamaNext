Making Up Your Own Mind ‚Äì Definition

What is Making Up Your Own Mind (MUYOM), is the question that this paper will answer. On a high-level, it is a minds-on book/course (by Edward Burger) that trains the reader how to apply the 5 Elements of Effective Thinking (5EET) to small puzzles with the hope that they will apply them to the larger puzzles in life. 5EET (also by Edward Burger) is a book that offers ways of thinking that involve deeper-levels of creativity and effectiveness than critical thinking. First, this paper will cover the basics: the different elements and how to use them, the structure of the puzzles in the book, and the critical hints provided that fosters alignment and growth. Secondly, it will cover my own opinionated applications of the elements to the books/software-engineering puzzles I faced. Lastly, it will conclude on the problems that have hindered my application of the concepts in the course in real-life. Join me, as we embark on one of the most life-changing systems I have ever adopted. 

The elements begin with Deep Understanding and end with Change; Deep Understanding in anything in life changes who we are as people. 

üå≥Earth is the element of Deep Understanding. Understanding is not a binary proposition but a spectrum and wherever we are we can always push ourselves to understand deeper. We can provoke our mind to think in an Earthly way with 3 prompts. The first is to start with the simple. The idea here is that by mastering the basics, we will naturally begin to understand the complexities of the given subject. Whenever facing a challenge, we should avoid it and probe a simpler problem in which we are grounded; in doing so, we will start to see patterns that exist both in the simple example and the original problem. True masters in any craft are those that have mastered the fundamentals. The book mentions a story regarding multiple trumpet players playing very complex & intricate pieces of work. After all of the trumpet players finished, the master then told them to play some very basic (almost child-like) pieces. Each sounded the same until the master began to play; the sound, the notes, and the tempo of his trumpet was so beautiful that it was clear who was the best ‚Äì something not as obvious on the more complex pieces. The second prompt is to spotlight the specific. The idea is to create a special case or specific example, probe it, and then recast whatever findings found to the larger problem. Being part of the Earth family, the example needs to be more simple than the original problem. Lastly, we have the most fluid prompt which is to add the adjective. It is exactly what it says: if we want to understand anything more deeply we should add an adjective (or descriptor) where we are to NOT leave for another descriptor until an insight is revealed. If the puzzle is deeply multi-faceted ‚Äì meaning, it may require multiple perspectives ‚Äì then this approach is ideal! Each of these prompts requires patience ‚Äî as we may believe that focusing on the basics is pointless ‚Äì and strategy ‚Äì like which examples should I create that would best expose the structure in the original problem or which adjective should I choose that would lead me to uncover the essential misunderstandings. 

üî•Fire is the element of failure; it is the most painful and rewarding approach in generating new insights. While we may not always know how to do something right, we certainly know how to do it wrong! Failing is the best teacher we have where we must hold onto each failed attempt as a precious joule until some new insight is realized. The beauty of failing is that it tells you exactly what did and did not work. Because each action we take will involve mostly failure & some success, this means that we should also learn from each of our applications of any element. There are 3 prompts we can use to provoke our minds to embrace it. The first prompt is to fail fast. We should never stare at a blank screen but instead write (or create) a rough draft where now we have something in which to respond too. When we care about failing we actually hinder our minds from being creative. There is actually a flow that happens when we don‚Äôt worry about failure which produces both junk & gems. We should be careful to not block this flow at all costs and to refine the result once it is done. We should inspect the result and see where we have a lack of understanding and fill in those gaps. The second prompt is to fail again. Imagine someone gives you a very difficult challenge and then tells you that it is a requirement that you fail 10x to succeed. The attitude now is, 1 down, 9 to go, we‚Äôve made progress! This prompt is both a call to be persistent & appreciative of failure. Many times when we approach a difficult challenge, we wait for the failure to occur rather than actively pursuing it as progress! This is a mindset change to pursue the goal of deep understanding rather than ‚Äúactual‚Äù progress in the challenge. We must realize that failure ‚Äì when learned from ‚Äì is indeed progress. Lastly, if we take the idea of failure seriously then we must also intentionally fail; we should create completely unrealistic scenarios. This approach allows us to think outside of the box and consider scenarios that we would not have otherwise. Once we have come up with an extreme-case, we then need to try taming it by analyzing the exact break-point and what may or may not have promise. Again, the objective with the extreme-case is to hold on to the failed attempt as a precious joule and create deeper understanding into the original issue. 

üí® Air is the element of creating questions. It is the most straight-forward approach into probing a puzzle and generating deeper understanding. This is NOT the act of asking a question but rather the art of continuously creating them. Wherever we are we should force ourselves to NOT be passive observers but rather be innately curious about the subject at hand; it‚Äôs a state of mind. The first prompt to provoke this state of mind is to be your own Socrates. Socrates asked the meta-questions, like why. A meta-question is a question that asks about the big picture structure or objective. It pushes us to ask, ‚ÄúAre we considering the right question?‚Äù since we may indeed be down the wrong route of reasoning. The second prompt is to ask a basic question. If we notice that the puzzle that we are facing requires fundamental knowledge that we lack, we must therefore ask fundamental questions for fundamental break-throughs. Lastly, the third prompt is to ask another question. We may be stuck on a puzzle and in order to refresh our thinking, we should ask another but related question. The idea is that if we can ask another question that is related to the puzzle‚Äôs question, we may direct our mind to provoke an insight around the original question. If no insight gets created, we can come back to the original puzzle more refreshed. 

üåäWater is the element of seeing the flow of ideas. Whenever we are trying to understand a subject deeper, we need to approach that process with the lens that each idea was derived from a prior idea and will transcend into a new idea. For example, when learning Calculus 1 we may fall prey into believing that each topic is standalone and unrelated to other topics. When we think like this, we often rely on memorization of formulas rather than asking ‚Äúwhy?‚Äù. Just like many subjects in life, Calculus 1 is deeply interconnected where the 2 main topics are derivatives and integrals. If we approach our learning knowing that, we will have an open mind to see the interconnected structure. The first prompt to provoke this mode of thinking is to run down all paths. This requires the determination to stick with one idea until it becomes obvious that it is a dead-end. When that is the case, we go down another path and do the same. As we do this, we are able to see which ideas don‚Äôt work and which ideas have promise. At each dead-end, we must ask why it is a dead-end and understand deeply. The second prompt is to Embrace doubt. We should understand that empathy and sympathy are not the same and consider alternative perspectives to the puzzle that we are facing. We should never be 100% sure about anything. This is the state of having an open-mind and being open to being wrong. Lastly, the third prompt is to never stop. We should always strive to see an idea flow to the very end. A lot of times, when we get a new idea we celebrate (rightfully so). However, the birth of a new idea is just the beginning where the real heavy-lifting comes from asking, ‚ÄúWhat‚Äôs next‚Äù? 

ü™®The Quintessential element is change. When we challenge ourselves to apply the first four elements of thinking, the puzzles themselves begin to change! Changing the way we see the puzzles in our lives is the purpose of the other elements. By applying ourselves to the struggle of the smaller puzzles ‚Äî practicing the four elements of thinking through them ‚Äì we will start to notice that our brain itself begins to change. What is change in reference to the puzzles? It is seeing the puzzle in a new light after applying the 4 different lenses to it. Every puzzle has a structure where the 4 elements of effective thinking help us to uncover it. When we see structure it is a Eureka moment, an insight that we get. Change goes all ways. The puzzle changes when we apply an elemental lens. The elemental part of our brain changes (that is the way we think) when we challenge ourselves to see the puzzle through each lens. However, the most important piece of change is that we start seeing the problems we face in life as puzzles. Life is a series of easy, medium, and hard puzzles that we mistakenly try to solve. We should not try to solve the puzzles in life but rather think through them and as a byproduct have it be solved. Solving the puzzles should NEVER be the goal, we should always try to think through them, interact with them, love them! When we love something we want to truly know and understand. And therefore, that is what we should do with the puzzles in our life and what the book offers. 


The Puzzles

We‚Äôve spoken about the 5 elements of effective thinking and their applications to the puzzles in the book. However, we have not truly discussed the puzzles in the book and how they were designed. A puzzle is a problem, game, or activity that is designed to test your ingenuity, thinking, or patience. When something is tested it either gets stronger or breaks. When we go to the gym, we test our muscles so that they become stronger. The pain we endure and the nutrition we consume after is essential for becoming stronger. After going to the gym on a consistent basis and being patient, only then will we notice that our body begins to change into a better version of ourselves. Those who benefit the most from the gym fall in love with the process. They do not do it for some means or gain but rather because they truly enjoy it. That joy does not come naturally & neither do we always enjoy the gym. It is a joy that we create. No one naturally loves pain. But everybody can always love the pain. Loving the pain takes action, effort, patience, and dedication. When it comes to the puzzles in the book, they are the weights in the mental gym. Just like a physical gym has a pull-up bar (for example) made for hanging and working out our arms, so does the mental gym have specific elemental weights for working out our mind. The weights are specific to the elements. For example, one puzzle may require greater empathy ‚Äî it is muli-fauceted ‚Äî which would require adding the adjective (Earth 3.0). But isn‚Äôt the objective to apply ALL the elements of effective thinking? That is correct. If we simply apply the 5 elements of effective thinking to the puzzles in our day to day life and never actually generate epiphanies or solutions, the elements have failed their purpose.
